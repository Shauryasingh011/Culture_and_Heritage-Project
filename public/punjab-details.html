Tradition

Tradition redefined, fashion reimagined.

Earlier: Punjabi men wore kurta–pyjama with phulkari-embroidered pagri (turban), while women wore salwar-kameez with colorful phulkari dupattas. Clothing was vibrant, reflecting the agricultural lifestyle.


Now: Modern wear like jeans, shirts, and western outfits are common. Salwar suits remain popular, while traditional phulkari is now worn mostly on weddings and festivals.


In short: Traditional attire has shifted from daily life to festive wear, blending with modern fashion while keeping cultural pride alive.

Ornaments

Elegance Wrapped
in Heritage

Punjabi jewellery was bold and intricate. Women adorned themselves with Chooda (red and white bangles worn by brides), Jhumkas (dangling earrings), Tikka on the forehead, Nath (nose ring), and Kadas (iron/steel bangles) symbolizing strength and faith.

Today, designs are lighter and modern, with gold and diamond pieces being popular, while traditional wedding jewellery still holds great importance.

Art

Crafting Culture, Creating Beauty

Punjab’s most celebrated art is Phulkari, the floral embroidery done with vibrant threads on shawls and dupattas, symbolizing love and prosperity. Handloom weaving, woodwork, and Punjabi miniature paintings also flourished in history.

Today, phulkari has found its place in global fashion, from sarees to home décor, keeping the cultural essence alive while adapting to modern trends.

Food

Taste the colors of India

Punjab’s cuisine is rich, hearty, and full of flavor. Traditional dishes include Sarson da Saag with Makke di Roti, Chole Bhature, Kadhi Pakora, and Lassi (buttermilk). Wheat, dairy, and ghee play a big role in daily meals. Sweets like Pinni, Jalebi, and Gur Wale Chawal are festival favorites.

Earlier, food was cooked in clay ovens (tandoors) and shared in communities. Even today, tandoori culture thrives worldwide as a symbol of Punjabi hospitality.

Folk Dance

Embrace the Rhythm of Indian Folk

Punjab is famous for its energetic and colorful folk dances. Bhangra, performed by men, celebrates harvest and joy with vigorous moves and beats of the dhol. Giddha, performed by women, expresses stories, humor, and traditions through graceful claps and lyrics. Jhumar is another graceful dance from the Sandalbar region.

These dances symbolize unity, festivity, and the agricultural heritage of Punjab, and are now performed worldwide as a symbol of Punjabi spirit.
