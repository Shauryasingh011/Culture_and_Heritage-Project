Kerala

Tradition redefined, culture celebrated.

Earlier: Kerala’s traditional attire was the Mundu (a white cloth with golden border) for men and the Set Saree (Kasavu) for women, worn with pride during festivals and daily life.


Now: While western clothes and modern sarees are common, the Kasavu Saree and Mundu are still worn during weddings, Onam, Vishu, and cultural events.


In short: Traditional dress has moved from everyday wear to cultural identity, celebrated especially during festivals.

Ornaments

Grace in Gold, Heritage in Design

Kerala jewellery is traditionally made of gold, reflecting prosperity and elegance. Women adorn themselves with Kasavu Mala, Manga Mala (mango-shaped chain), Oddiyanam (waist belt), Elakkathali (neckpiece) and Jhimkis (earrings).

Today, lighter versions and modern designs are popular, but during weddings and festivals, women still wear heavy layers of traditional ornaments. Gold remains a cultural symbol of wealth and pride in Kerala.

Art

Colors of Faith, Expressions of Culture

Kerala is famous for Kathakali, a classical dance-drama known for elaborate makeup and storytelling. Another unique art form is Theyyam, a ritual performance blending dance, drama, and devotion.

Murals, coir crafts, and handloom weaving also form an important part of Kerala’s artistic identity. These art forms are not just entertainment but spiritual expressions connecting people to culture and tradition.

Food

Flavors of Coconut & Spices

Kerala cuisine is a blend of spices, coconut, and rice. The famous Sadya (traditional feast) is served on a banana leaf with dishes like Sambar, Avial, Thoran, Olan, Pachadi, and Payasam.

Other popular dishes include Appam with stew, Malabar Parotta, Puttu with Kadala Curry, Kerala Fish Curry, and Beef Fry. Use of coconut milk, curry leaves, and spices makes Kerala food aromatic and unique.

Folk Dance

Dance of Devotion & Celebration

Kerala’s folk dances reflect its devotion, festivals, and social life. Thiruvathira is performed by women in circles during Onam. Mohiniyattam expresses grace and love through slow movements. Theyyam combines ritual, dance, and vibrant costumes, performed in temples.

Other dances include Oppana (wedding dance), Kolkali (stick dance), and Duffmuttu (performed by Muslim communities). Each form is tied deeply to Kerala’s heritage and religious traditions.
